# Sound-Reacted-Magnetic-Fluid

This is a project for the Magnetic fluid microphone

1. Experimental Objectives and System Overview
The goal of this project is to design and produce a ferrofluid-based visual interactive device that changes dynamically with the rhythm of music. This project integrates multiple disciplines, including acoustic signal processing, microcontroller algorithm application, and electromagnetic physics.

Phase 1: Audio Signal Acquisition and Hardware Conditioning (Time-Domain Signal Input)
The initial step of the experiment involves capturing ambient acoustic data. The system utilizes a MAX9814 microphone module integrated with a high-sensitivity electret microphone. To verify its operational status, a dedicated audio testing script was developed beforehand to output analog voltages via the serial port in real time, ensuring hardware integrity. When music is played externally, the microphone instantly converts the acoustic waves into an analog voltage signal that fluctuates drastically over time (i.e., a time-domain signal).

Given the presence of ambient background noise in open air, an internal Automatic Gain Control (AGC) circuit within the MAX9814 is fully leveraged to prevent control distortion. The AGC functions as an intelligent volume regulator: it automatically amplifies weak signals when ambient music is low and dynamically compresses the amplitude when the volume peaks. Through this hardware conditioning, the waveform is perfectly constrained within the optimal safety range for the microcontroller’s ADC sampling (0V - 5V). This process not only enhances the system's sensitivity to faint bass beats but also prevents signal clipping caused by overdriving at high volumes, thereby establishing a clean data foundation for subsequent precise mathematical analysis.

Phase 2: Microsecond-Level Clock Sampling and Fast Fourier Transform (Mathematical Transition from Time to Frequency Domain)
Once the conditioned voltage signal enters the Arduino Pro Mini microcontroller, the core mathematical processing of this project begins. To reconstruct an accurate audio spectrum, the program defines a sampling frequency of 10 kHz. In strict accordance with signal processing requirements, the time interval between sampling points must be absolutely uniform. Therefore, utilizing the microcontroller's internal high-precision clock and deterministic while loops, the system forces a strict timing constraint to capture a voltage data point precisely every 100 microseconds, continuously collecting 128 sampling points to form a complete analysis cycle.

Upon gathering all 128 points, the code immediately invokes the arduinoFFT library. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm acts as a mathematical scalpel; its essence lies in decomposing complex time-domain waveforms into a superposition of countless pure sine and cosine waves of varying frequencies and amplitudes. Through this accelerated computation, the signal instantly transitions from the time axis to the frequency axis.
Because ferrofluid responds sluggishly to high frequencies such as human vocals, the algorithm isolates and accumulates the energy values within the specific low-frequency band of 60 Hz - 250 Hz (Bass/Bass Drum) upon completing the transform. Utilizing the map() function, this abstract bass energy is proportionally mapped to a digital PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) duty cycle signal, providing a quantified digital metric for the subsequent physical actuation.

Phase 3: Weak-Current Controls Strong-Current Power Amplification and Thermal Management Safety (Electromagnetic Drive Stage)
Although the microcontroller executes sophisticated algorithms, the power delivery capability of its GPIO pins is extremely weak (5V, ~20mA), making it entirely incapable of driving the high-power 12V/1000mA DC electromagnet. Consequently, an IRF520 MOSFET module is introduced as a power bridge between the micro-control logic and the high-power hardware.
The Gate of the MOSFET receives the high-frequency PWM signal from the microcontroller. As the musical rhythm fluctuates in intensity, the PWM duty cycle shifts dynamically between 0 and 200. Operating akin to a high-frequency valve, the MOSFET switches states thousands of times per second, precisely modulating the magnitude of the current delivered from the external independent 12V power supply into the electromagnet.

At this stage, engineering safety and "transistor explosion" prevention are paramount. Because the electromagnet consists of tightly wound inductive coils, the instant the MOSFET rapidly cuts off the current, the coil generates a Back Electromotive Force (Back EMF) reaching up to several hundred volts. To mitigate this, a flyback diode is connected in parallel with reverse polarity across the electromagnet to guide and safely dissipate the high-voltage spike within a closed loop. Concurrently, a hard-coded power limit (capping the maximum PWM duty cycle) and an idle-cooling logic—which cuts off power if no music is detected for 5 seconds—are implemented in the software, ensuring long-term thermal stability and hardware safety for the high-power system.

Phase 4: Visual Manifestation of Rosensweig Instability (Physical Actuation Stage)
Ultimately, the modulated current flows through the coil, exciting a spatially non-uniform, alternating magnetic field at the base of the glass container. This sets the stage for the physical finale of the ferrofluid (a superparamagnetic fluid).
In the absence of a magnetic field, the nanoscale magnetic particles within the fluid are randomly distributed, settling at the bottom of the container due to gravity. However, when the underlying electromagnet unleashes a strong magnetic field in sync with the musical beat, the superparamagnetic nanoparticles rapidly align along the magnetic field lines. At this juncture, the magnetic gradient force (which attempts to stretch the fluid) engages in an intense spatial competition against the surface tension and gravity of the liquid (which attempt to compress and pull it down). When the magnetic field intensity crosses a critical threshold due to surging bass energy, the surface equilibrium of the fluid breaks down, macroscopically erupting into a highly regular array of conical spikes—a phenomenon renowned in physics as "Rosensweig Instability."

Driven by the rhythmic cadence of the music's low frequency, the spatial energy density of the magnetic field rises and falls dynamically. In response, the ferrofluid stretches, spikes, and contracts with extreme precision. Ultimately, the abstract audio electrical signals are flawlessly transformed into a dynamic, compelling, and visually stunning electromagnetic physical display.
